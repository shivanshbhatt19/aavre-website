Udaipur to Kumbhalgarh by cab: the Great Wall of India in a day

By Aavre    July 2026    7 min read

Some places make you understand a piece of history the moment you see them. Kumbhalgarh is one of those. You round a bend in the Aravalli hills and there it is, a wall that runs for kilometres along the ridgeline, rising and falling with the land. People call it the Great Wall of India, and once you have stood on it you understand why.

It is one of the best day trips you can do from Udaipur, and it is more manageable than most visitors expect. Here is how to plan it.

How far is Kumbhalgarh from Udaipur?

Kumbhalgarh is about 85 kilometres north of Udaipur. The drive takes roughly 2 to 2.5 hours each way. Unlike the highway run to Chittorgarh, this route climbs into the Aravalli hills, and the final stretch winds through villages and up towards the fort. It is a beautiful drive, but the last section is slower going, so build a little extra time into your plans.

Most people do Kumbhalgarh comfortably as a day trip, leaving Udaipur in the morning and returning in the evening. If you want to see the evening light and sound show at the fort, an overnight stay nearby is worth considering, but it is not necessary to see the place well.

The drive itself

The journey takes you out of the city and into quieter, greener country. The road runs through the Rajsamand district and gradually gains height as the hills close in around you. The most common route goes via Kelwara, the small town at the foot of the fort, and the scenery through here is some of the prettiest in the region.

The one thing to keep in mind is the final approach. The last stretch of road climbs and winds, with some sharp turns as you near the fort. It is perfectly manageable in a comfortable car with an experienced driver, but it is not a road to rush.

During the monsoon the hills around Kumbhalgarh turn green and the drive is at its most beautiful, but the winding final stretch can get slippery after heavy rain. Start early, avoid driving the hill road after dark and let your driver set the pace on the climb.

The fort and the wall

Kumbhalgarh Fort is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most impressive fortifications anywhere in India. It was built in the fifteenth century by Rana Kumbha, took around fifteen years to complete and sits high in the Aravalli range. It is also the birthplace of Maharana Pratap, the Mewar ruler whose name still carries enormous weight in Rajasthan.

The wall is the thing everyone comes for. It runs for roughly 36 kilometres, which makes it one of the longest continuous walls in the world and the reason for its nickname, the Great Wall of India. In places it is wide enough for several people to walk abreast, and standing on it with the hills falling away on both sides is the kind of view that stays with you.

What to see inside

Walk a stretch of the wall

You will not walk all 36 kilometres, but you can walk a good stretch of the ramparts near the fort, and it is the single best thing to do here. The scale only makes sense when you are on it, looking along the wall as it snakes over the ridgeline into the distance.

Badal Mahal, the Palace of Clouds

At the highest point of the fort sits Badal Mahal. The climb up rewards you with panoramic views across the Aravallis, and on a clear day you can see for a very long way. The palace itself is worth exploring for its rooms and the sense of just how high you have come.

The temples and the Neelkanth Mahadev

The fort holds more than 360 temples within its walls, a remarkable number for a single fortification. The Neelkanth Mahadev temple, dedicated to Shiva, is among the most notable and remains an active place of worship. Wandering between the shrines gives you a feel for how complete a world this fort once was.

Combining Kumbhalgarh with Ranakpur

If you are willing to make a longer day of it, Kumbhalgarh pairs beautifully with the Ranakpur Jain temple, which lies further along in the same direction. Ranakpur is one of the most stunning temple complexes in India, carved entirely in white marble with over a thousand pillars, no two alike. Many travellers do the two together, and it makes for a rich, full day.

How much time you need

Give yourself two to three hours at the fort to walk the wall, climb to Badal Mahal and see the main temples without rushing. Add the drive at each end, and Kumbhalgarh fills a comfortable day. If you are adding Ranakpur, plan for an early start and a full day out.

Best time to visit

October through March is the ideal season, with cool, dry weather that makes the wall walk and the climb genuinely enjoyable. The Kumbhalgarh Festival, held at the fort in the winter months, is worth timing a trip around if you can. The monsoon brings the hills to life in green, though you will want to plan around the rain and the slower drive. Summer is best tackled with early starts, before the midday heat settles on the ramparts.
