Udaipur to Ranakpur by cab: the temple of 1,444 pillars

By Aavre    July 2026    6 min read

Some buildings are famous for their size, others for their setting. The Ranakpur Jain temple is famous for its detail. Step inside and you are surrounded by 1,444 marble pillars, and the line everyone repeats is that no two are carved alike. Sunlight moves through them across the day and the whole hall seems to shift with it. It is one of the most beautiful temples in India, and one of the best day trips from Udaipur.

It takes a little planning, mostly around when tourists are allowed in, and this guide covers all of it.

How far is Ranakpur from Udaipur?

Ranakpur is about 90 kilometres northwest of Udaipur, tucked into a quiet valley in the Aravalli hills in Pali district. The drive takes roughly 2 to 2.5 hours each way. Most travellers go via Gogunda and Sayra, a scenic route through forested hills, and the roads are mostly good with a few narrower, winding stretches as you near the temple.

It sits on the road between Udaipur and Jodhpur, which is why so many people fold it into a longer Rajasthan journey. As a day trip from Udaipur it is very doable, and it pairs famously with Kumbhalgarh.

The drive itself

The route north from Udaipur climbs gently into the Aravallis, passing through small villages and green, forested country that feels a world away from the city. It is a pretty drive, calmer than the highways, though the last stretch near Ranakpur narrows and winds a little, so it is not a road to hurry. Tolls on this route are minimal.

This is the one thing to plan around. Tourists are allowed inside the temple only after the morning prayer rituals, usually from around 12 noon until about 5 pm, with mornings reserved for worship. If you arrive too early you will wait, so time your departure from Udaipur to reach around midday. There is also a dress code, shoulders and knees covered, and leather items such as belts and wallets are not allowed inside.

The Ranakpur Jain temple

The temple, properly the Chaumukha or four faced temple, was built in the fifteenth century by a Jain businessman named Dharna Shah, on land granted by Rana Kumbha of Mewar. It is dedicated to Adinatha, the first Tirthankara, and the four faced idol looks out in all four directions, a symbol of the universal reach of the faith. It is counted among the five most important pilgrimage sites in Jainism.

What everyone remembers is the marble. The whole complex is carved from pale marble, three storeys of it spread across a huge base, held up by those 1,444 pillars. The carving is astonishingly fine, dancers and patterns worked into the stone, and the light through the hall gives the marble a soft, changing glow. A local guide is genuinely worth it here, since much of the finest detail is easy to walk straight past.

How much time you need

Give yourself two to three hours to do the temple justice, wandering the halls without rushing. With the noon opening for tourists, a comfortable plan is to leave Udaipur mid morning, reach Ranakpur for the start of visiting hours, spend the afternoon there and drive back in the evening.

Combining Ranakpur with Kumbhalgarh

Ranakpur and Kumbhalgarh sit only about 50 kilometres apart, and together they make one of the finest day trips in the region. The classic plan is Kumbhalgarh in the morning, for the wall walk and the fort before the heat, then Ranakpur in the afternoon once the temple opens to visitors. It is a full day, but a rewarding one, and it is a route we run often.

Best time to visit

October through March is the ideal window, with cool, pleasant weather for both the drive and the temple. The marble stays cool underfoot even in warmer months, but the drive and the wait for the noon opening are far more comfortable in winter. Whenever you go, carry water and plan around that midday opening.
